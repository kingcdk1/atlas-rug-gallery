The Story of Hassan Khoshrou Since 1986

A Persian story, told in wool.

Atlas Rug Gallery began with one man who could not stop loving the rugs of his homeland. Forty years later, his three sons carry on what he built. This is the story of Hassan Khoshrou, and of the beautiful pieces that brought him to Fort Worth, Texas.

The First Generation

Hassan came from a place where rugs were not decoration.

In Persia, a rug was not something you bought for a room. A rug was where your grandfather prayed in the morning. It was where your cousins were born. It was where the family gathered after dinner, poured tea, and told stories late into the night. A rug was the floor of a life.

Hassan Khoshrou grew up in that world. He learned the names of weaves before he learned arithmetic. He could tell a Kashan from an Isfahan by the smell of the wool. When he left his homeland for America in [YEAR], he did not leave that world behind. He packed it up and brought it with him.

Fort Worth, 1986

A small space on Camp Bowie.

In 1986, Hassan opened the doors of Atlas Rug Gallery with a modest inventory, a handshake guarantee, and a conviction that Fort Worth would come to love these pieces the way his own family did. He was right. Within a year, the gallery was a gathering place.

Customers came for rugs and stayed for the stories. Hassan poured tea, walked them across the floor, and showed them what to look for. The pattern that told you where a rug was born. The knot density that told you how long a weaver had spent on it. The small imperfections that proved human hands had made it. Few people left without learning something.

He never sold you a rug. He taught you how to see one. A Longtime Customer

The Great Teacher

He was a teacher, first.

Hassan was a rug dealer on paper. What he actually did was teach. He taught young couples how to pick a rug that would grow with them. He taught designers how to read a weave like a signature. He taught collectors how to tell a city piece from a village piece at thirty paces. And quietly, he taught every one of his customers something about themselves: take your time, trust your eye, and live with beautiful things.

He believed beauty made you healthier. He believed hurry made you smaller. He believed a good rug, a pot of tea, and an hour with a friend was worth more than most of what the world called important. He was probably right.

The Joy

The laughter was as good as the rugs.

Anyone who walked into Atlas Rug Gallery in any decade since 1986 will tell you the same thing. Hassan made you laugh. Not a salesman's laugh. A real one. He had a story for every rug, a joke for every occasion, and a way of making complete strangers feel like old friends.

Many of those strangers became exactly that. Three generations of Fort Worth families now count their visits to Hassan as part of their own family memory. Children who played on the gallery floor in the 80s come in now with children of their own, looking for a piece to pass down.

Every Rug a Tale

These are not floor coverings. They are stories.

Ask any of the sons. They know the ones Hassan loved most.

01 The Isfahan

Never for sale.

A fine Isfahan piece has hung on the back wall of the gallery since the first week. Every dealer has one like it. The rug you would sooner close the store than part with. Hassan bought it before he opened the doors and never changed his mind.

02 The Kashan

Cleaned on the house.

A grieving widow once brought in a large Kashan her husband had loved. She asked how much to clean it. Hassan said, next time, bring pastries. She still brings rugs in every year. She still brings pastries.

03 The Heriz

Three generations deep.

A young couple bought a tribal Heriz in 1989 for their first home. Thirty years later, their daughter walked into the gallery looking for one of her own. She did not know her parents had done the same thing in the same room.

04 The Prayer Rug

Beside the desk.

A small old piece from Qum has always sat beside Hassan's desk. He will not sell it. He says it keeps him honest. When you visit, ask about it. The sons will tell you the story.

The gallery holds a thousand more like these. Come hear them in person.

What He Taught Us

Four lessons, forty years in.

  1. 01

    A rug is a relationship.

    Hassan never understood transactional selling. Every rug on the floor had a family it belonged to, and his job was to find that family. The sons still sell this way. It takes longer. It is worth it.

  2. 02

    Beauty is not a luxury.

    He believed beautiful things made you live better, sleep deeper, love harder, and feel more alive. A good rug was not a splurge. It was a small daily medicine. Four decades of customers will tell you he was right.

  3. 03

    Take your time.

    Hassan never pushed a sale. Sit with the rug. Sleep on it. Come back next week. A rug picked quickly is a rug regretted slowly. A rug picked well is a rug loved forever.

  4. 04

    Laugh more than you sell.

    Rugs are serious. People do not have to be. The joy of walking into this gallery has always been half the reason people came back. We are still committed to that part. Come laugh with us.
